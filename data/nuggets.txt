Season Overview
The Denver Nuggets posted a 54–28 record, finishing as the third seed in the West and reinforcing what the league has known for several years — that as long as Nikola Jokic is on the floor, Denver is a championship-caliber team. Their season was defined by efficiency, intelligence, and the relentless brilliance of their center.
Strengths
Denver's offense is the most sophisticated in the league, built entirely around Jokic's passing, scoring, and decision-making from the elbow and post. No team forces more difficult defensive decisions per possession than the Nuggets. Their ability to execute late-game situations is among the best in the NBA.
Weaknesses
Depth and athleticism are Denver's recurring concerns. Against teams that can push pace and attack their wings in space, the Nuggets can be exposed defensively. Their title window is also tied closely to Jokic's health, and without him they are a dramatically different — and lesser — team.
Key Players
Nikola Jokic is the unquestioned centerpiece — a three-time MVP who sees the game at a level no other player in the world currently matches. Jamal Murray provides the secondary star power and clutch-shooting ability the Nuggets need when playoff series tighten. Together they form one of the NBA's most dangerous and proven duos.
Coaching Analysis
Michael Malone has built a culture of player ownership and tactical intelligence in Denver. His trust in Jokic to essentially co-coach the offense in real time is unique league-wide, and his defensive schemes have steadily improved to complement the team's offensive brilliance.
Playoff Outlook
Denver is arguably the West's most dangerous third seed. Their playoff experience, championship DNA, and Jokic's supernatural ability to elevate in high-stakes games make them a legitimate title threat. No team in the West wants to draw the Nuggets in a seven-game series.
